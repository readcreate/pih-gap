Understanding Gap Analysis

Before we collect data, let's align on the conceptual framework. Click through each concept to explore.

Landscape Analysis

What Exists?

A broad survey of current programs, policies, actors, and resources. It asks: "Who is doing what, where?"


Focus: Descriptive inventory of existing MCH services, funding streams, and stakeholders.

Gap Analysis

What's Missing?

A targeted assessment comparing what exists to what is needed. It asks: "Where are the critical unmet needs?"


Focus: Prescriptive. Identifies specific deficits in quality, coverage, or capacity to set actionable priorities.

Why this matters for PIH — Example: A landscape analysis tells us there are 12 health facilities in a catchment area. A gap analysis tells us that 9 lack skilled birth attendants and that this gap drives 60% of preventable newborn deaths — and must be the top priority.

From Data → Decision → Accountability

Evidence-informed decision-making means systematically gathering the best available data — quantitative, qualitative, and community knowledge — to set priorities that are defensible, transparent, and trackable.

The Priority Matrix helps us invest limited resources where they will have the greatest impact. We score gaps on two axes: Severity (how critical is it?) and Feasibility (can we act on it now?).

Example: EmOC is critically understaffed (high severity) AND the Ministry has a nurse redeployment program available (high feasibility) → Act Now.
